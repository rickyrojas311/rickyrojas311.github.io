Paper Planes

Deconstructing the complexities of US flight delays through a paper-animated lens

Growing up I spent countless hours watching infotainment YouTubers. From science to history to random, niche facts, I learned so much about the world through well-researched, well-produced, and (most importantly) accessible videos. I credit these videos to contributing a large part to my love of science. Channels like Wendover Productions, 3Blue1Brown, or Vox could take complex subjects and easily distill them into their most important points.

When I took PWR91CW: Seeing is Believing: The Power of Persuasive Data Stories, I knew that I wanted to try my hand at the medium that had such a big impact on me and produce an explainer-style video for our final project. Mimicking a Vox style, we presented our data analysis on flight delays in the US, exploring reasons for the delays, how they’ve changed overtime, and potential solutions to the problems identified.

We chose to replicate a Vox paper-and-pen style of animated data visualization for two reasons. Firstly, it was accessible. As scientists we don’t (usually) have access to a post-production team nor the ability to create fancy data animations. However, by manually animating the video with paper cut-outs, it allows us to access many of the same benefits to animated video (such as being able to strictly direct the viewers’ attention to certain things at a given timepoint). Secondly, the paper-and-pen style projects a sense of familiarity and intimacy with the topic. Our message isn’t overly polished or corporate; it’s data by the everyday person for the everyday person.

In this project I mainly worked on fine tuning of the data visualizations and the initial data analysis. While we all had input in the form of the final visualizations, I was in charge of making sure that they were suitable for a video and matched the style.

Creating an explainer-style video was a full-circle moment for me. After years of being a student of YouTube's best science communicators, I finally sat on the other side of the screen. This experience pushed me to think of how I can present data in whole new ways.
