Attention-guided encoding

A long form scientific endeavor on language processing in the brain

Over the last year I’ve been working on a project for my senior thesis and it’s truly been a culmination of everything I’ve learned in my undergraduate degree. From the communication skills I acquired in the NSC to the computational skills from my data science classes, it’s all been put to the test. With the help of my research advisor I wrote, submitted, and received grant funding for my project and have been working on it for nearly a year.

This project forced me to engage deeply with every step of the research process. I started with broad idea formulation, exploring how we might bridge the gap between computational representations of language—like the attention mechanisms in large language models—and traditional human linguistic structures. The early months were spent submerged in the literature, coming up with hypotheses and replicating prior findings. It wasn't a linear path; I frequently hit dead ends, got stuck on tricky data representations, and had to pivot constantly to find new approaches to extract meaning from our results.

Sharing my preliminary findings during our weekly lab meetings was one of the most challenging but rewarding aspects of this process. When you spend months staring at the same code, procedures, and data, the concepts become so deeply familiar to you that it’s incredibly easy to skip over crucial context. Presenting my progress to the rest of the lab forced me to step back and re-evaluate my narrative. I had to defend my methodology, and having my ideas actively challenged by other researchers was a humbling reminder of why science communication is so vital. You can't just rely on the assumption that the data speaks for itself; you have to build a resilient, logical argument that can withstand expert scrutiny while remaining accessible enough to follow.

There are many visualizations I made to explain my methods but I am particularly proud of my explanation of the model architecture of BERT (slides 14-18). I heard from other members of the lab that model architecture explanations can be very confusing and that they felt I did a particularly good job. I believe that this largely comes from trying to keep the slides simple and focused by spreading out the steps across multiple slides instead of cramming it into one complex visualization.

Ultimately, this artifact represents the true synthesis of my time at Stanford. It proves that being a good data scientist isn't just about training models or running statistical tests, it’s about having the perseverance to find the answers hidden within the numbers, and the communication skills to share those discoveries with the world.
