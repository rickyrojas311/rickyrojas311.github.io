Teaching Data Storytelling

Cultivating a deeper understanding of data storytelling by redesigning introductory coursework to prioritize narrative clarity

I’ve been a teaching assistant for Data Science 112: Principles of Data Science for 4 quarters—beginning with my sophomore year. I’ve been with the course the whole time it’s been offered at Stanford and have watched it change overtime. I’ve witnessed how it’s been adapted to withstand advancements in generative AI, altered to focus on the most critical skills for introductory data science students, and I’ve developed a very good understanding of the concepts students typically struggle with. As I progressed through the Notation in Science Communication, I was able to develop and refine communication skills as a teacher. Thinking about how to break down abstract concepts and reformulate them into simple, presentable ways isn’t just a science communication concept but lies at the heart of teaching.

One area I repeatedly saw students struggle in was data visualization. Though they were able to create charts suitable for the data type and used different features such as size or color to convey information in a compact way, they struggled situating their visualization in more complex situations. For example, at the end of the quarter students create a poster with the results of their final project. Students would spend a long time on their projects and find many very interesting results, but when it came time to present their project they would struggle with formulating a “story” which was supported by their visualizations. Students would frequently make visualizations without a clear purpose which took a while to decipher, or they would try to cram too much information into one visualization.

After working with the instructor of the course and another TA, we came up with a plan to help encourage students to think more critically about data visualizations over the course of the quarter. At the beginning of every class, the professor would display a “bad” data visualization and go over some of the problems with it as well as some potential fixes. This way students could see how poor plotting practices could be misleading, confusing, or otherwise unhelpful and get a clearer idea of how to fix some of the issues.

Additionally, I worked with another TA to rewrite one of the homework assignments for the course. Originally the assignment, which has students analyzing companies who received deals on the popular TV show Shark Tank, required students to make a few complex charts using different features, but we didn’t ask students to think too much about what purpose their chart was supposed to serve. To remedy this, we reframed the assignment around a central question—” How can you maximize your chance of success on Shark Tank?”—guiding students through the full data pipeline from exploratory analysis to purposeful visualization. We forced students to explicitly state the “takeaway” or message they were going to highlight before making their chart.

The impact of these changes was immediately visible during the final poster sessions. Rather than presenting a cluttered dump of data, students began to treat their visualizations as the backbone of a logical argument. I noticed a significant shift in how they approached their final projects; they were no longer just plotting variables but were actively selecting the right visual tools to voice their findings. By forcing that moment of reflection, asking them to define the "takeaway" before touching the code, we helped them transition from being data processors to data storytellers.

Refining this curriculum also served as a catalyst for my own development within the Notation in Science Communication. It taught me that effective teaching isn't just about transferring technical knowledge; it’s about anticipating the cognitive hurdles of your audience. Whether I was identifying the pitfalls of a "bad" graph or helping a student simplify a complex scatter plot, I was practicing the core tenet of science communication: making the complex accessible without losing its integrity. My time with Data Science 112 has shown me that the most powerful data in the world is only as good as the story you can tell with it.
